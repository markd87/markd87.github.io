Research

(Email at Manchester: mark.danovich@postgrad.manchester.ac.uk)

My research at the national graphene institute is focused on the electronic and optoelectronic properties of atomic two-dimensional (2D) materials and their heterostructure.
Since the discovery of the magic material graphene (single layer of graphite) in 2004 at the University of Manchester, people have applied similar methods to isolate single layers of other stacked three-dimensional materials and study them. These resulted in a wide variety of materials with a wide range of properties and potential applications. From the technological point-of-view, the possibility of having atom-thin electronic devices, with superior characteristics as compared to modern day materials used in the industry, and the ability to tune the desired properties of devices by combining different monolayers in a lego-like manner, is very promising and revolutionary. From the theoretical point-of-view, these 2D materials provide an exciting playground for condensed matter theory studies to explore new and exotic phenomena unique to these ideal 2D systems.

A particular family of such materials which gained a lot of attention and interest due to their own unique properties, are the transition metal dichalcogenides, which my research focuses on.

2D Transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDCs)

TMDCs are a class of two dimensional materials consisting of a base unit of a transition metal (most commonly Mo and W) and two chalcogen atoms (most commonly S, Se) forming a hexagonal lattice.



Left: Artistic view (created by me using Blender) of a TMDC monolayer with the purple spheres representing the metal atoms, and the yellow spheres the chalcogen atoms. Each metal atom is connected to 3 chalcogen atoms above and below, forming a trigonal prism. Right: Top view of the TMDC showing the hexagonal lattice composed of metal and chalcogen atoms.


TMDCs have unique electronic properties which together with the two dimensional confinement offer a wide range of phenomena to be studied with implications for both condensed matter physics and applications in the fields of electronics, optotelectronics, and spintronics. These properties include: optical range direct band-gap, relatively large exciton and trion binding energies, strong spin-orbit coupling resulting in large spin splitting (on the order of ~100 meV), and broken inversion symmetry.

Topics I have worked on include:

1. The relaxation of photo-excited carriers in TMDCs through the emission of phonons [arXiv, DOI]

In this project we have shown that hot carriers (electrons/holes) in TMDCs relax on ps time scales through emission of optical phonons. This fast relaxation/cooling of the carriers is of great importance for applications in optoelectronics.


Poster presented at Graphene2016 conference in Genoa, Italy (19-22/4/2016)

2. Non-radiative relaxation of excitons/trions in WS2 and WSe2 monolayers [arXiv, 2D materials - DOI]

In this project we propose a novel non-radiative relaxation process for excitons/trions in TMDCs, giving a plausible explanation for the observed low quantum efficiencies
